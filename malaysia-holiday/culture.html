Culture & Heritage

A Melting Pot of Asia

Harmonious Diversity

Malaysia's greatest asset is its cultural diversity. For centuries, the Malay Peninsula and Borneo served as central trade links between East and West Asia, resulting in a rich blending of ethnicities, customs, religions, and food.

The population is comprised mainly of **Malays, Chinese, Indians**, and a rich tapestry of **indigenous ethnic groups** in Sabah and Sarawak. This multi-ethnic society lives together in harmony, resulting in a vibrant calendar of festivals celebrated by all.

"Muhibbah" is a beautiful Malay term that describes the spirit of unity and harmony between diverse communities living together under one flag.

Malay Heritage

Forming the majority of the population, Malays speak Bahasa Melayu, practice Islam, and are known for their warm hospitality, traditional batik fabrics, silat martial arts, and ornate wooden architecture.

Chinese Heritage

Arriving in large numbers in the 19th century, Malaysian Chinese practice Buddhism, Taoism, and Christianity. They have heavily influenced trade, architecture, and food, and are famous for the Peranakan (Nyonya) culture.

Indian Heritage

Mostly of Tamil descent, Malaysian Indians practice Hinduism, Islam, and Christianity. Their presence is marked by beautiful temples, vibrant sari wear, classical dances, and the beloved Mamak food stalls.

East Malaysian Tribes

Sabah and Sarawak are home to over 30 indigenous groups including the Iban, Kadazan-Dusun, and Bidayuh. They possess unique languages, elaborate beadwork, bamboo dances, and historic longhouses.

A Calendar of Celebrations

Because of its multicultural nature, Malaysia celebrates festivals all year round. Here are the major national holidays.

Festival Celebrated By Approximate Timing Key Cultural Traditions
Hari Raya Aidilfitri Muslims (Malay community) Shifting (Based on Islamic calendar) Family reunions, forgiveness prayers, wearing traditional Baju Melayu/Kurung, "Open House" feasts with rendang and ketupat.
Chinese New Year Chinese community January or February Red lanterns, lion dances, reunion dinners, giving ang pow (red envelopes), Yee Sang tossing for prosperity.
Deepavali (Diwali) Hindus (Indian community) October or November Lighting clay lamps (diyas), drawing colorful kolam (rice designs) on the floor, family prayers, sharing sweet Indian delicacies.
Gawai Dayak & Kaamatan Dayak (Sarawak) & Kadazan (Sabah) May and June Harvest festivals celebrated with traditional dances (Ngajat), drinking tuak (rice wine), and giving thanks to rice spirits.
